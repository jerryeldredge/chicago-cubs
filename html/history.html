Chicago Cubs History

The Chicago Cubs have a rich and storied history in Major League Baseball. The team was founded in 1876 and has been a part of the National League since its inception. The Cubs have experienced both triumphs and challenges throughout their history, making them one of the most beloved teams in baseball.

One of the most significant moments in Cubs history came in 2016 when they won the World Series, ending a 108-year championship drought. This victory was celebrated by fans around the world and solidified the Cubs' place in baseball history. The team has also had many notable players over the years, including Ernie Banks, who is considered one of the greatest players in Cubs history, and Ryne Sandberg, who was known for his exceptional defensive skills.

The Cubs have also had their share of challenges, including periods of poor performance and management issues. However, their loyal fan base has remained steadfast throughout the years, supporting the team through thick and thin. The Cubs continue to be a beloved part of Chicago's sports culture and have a rich tradition that continues to thrive today.

  3. Notable Players
    • Cap Anson — Anson joined Chicago in 1876 and went on to win four batting titles, led the NL in RBIs eight times, and is the franchise's all-time leader in hits, runs, RBIs, and doubles.
    • Hack Wilson — Wilson's bat was thunderous, and his 1930 season remains legendary with 56 home runs and 191 RBIs, records that stood for decades, making him one of the most dominant sluggers of his era.
    • Gabby Hartnett — A six-time All-Star, Hartnett spent 19 of his 20 MLB seasons with the Cubs, totaling a career-high 37 home runs and 122 RBIs in 1930 and earning the 1935 NL MVP.
    • Ernie Banks ("Mr. Cub") — A two-time National League MVP, Banks hit 512 home runs during his career and was the first player in Cubs history to have his number retired.
    • Billy Williams ("Sweet Swingin' Billy") — A six-time All-Star and the 1972 NL batting champion, Williams combined smooth power with remarkable consistency, becoming one of the Cubs' most reliable hitters.
    • Ron Santo — A nine-time All-Star, Santo was known for his exceptional defense at third base and his clutch hitting, spending 14 seasons with the Cubs before being inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame in 2012.
    • Ferguson Jenkins — Jenkins became the franchise's all-time leader in WAR among pitchers, strikeouts, and games started, winning the 1971 NL Cy Young Award and was inducted into the Hall of Fame in 1991.
    • Ryne Sandberg — With 10 All-Star selections, nine Gold Gloves, and an MVP in 1984, he became one of the most complete players in Cubs history, combining defense, speed, and clutch hitting.
    • Andre Dawson ("The Hawk") — Dawson led the big leagues in 1987 with 49 homers and 137 RBI, earning a Gold Glove Award, a Silver Slugger Award, and the National League MVP.
    • Greg Maddux — Widely regarded as one of the greatest pitchers of all time, Maddux won the Cy Young Award in 1992 and 1993 as a Cub and won five Gold Gloves with the team during his two stints in Chicago.
    • Sammy Sosa — Sosa hit 545 home runs as a Cub, the most in franchise history, and was a seven-time All-Star best known for his historic home run race with Mark McGwire in 1998, finishing the season with 66 home runs.
    • Kris Bryant — At 24, Bryant helped the Cubs snap a 108-year World Series drought, winning the National League MVP by homering 39 times in 2016, on top of winning the Rookie of the Year Award the prior season.
  4. Cubs Culture & Traditions
    • The Hand-Operated Scoreboard (1937) — Unlike most modern stadiums, Wrigley Field's scoreboard in center field is still manually operated. First installed in 1937, workers physically change numbers from inside, maintaining an old-school charm rare in today's high-tech ballparks.
    • The Ivy-Covered Walls (1937) — The original vines were purchased and planted by Bill Veeck in September 1937, and Wrigley Field remains the only major league ballpark with ivy-covered outfield walls.
    • The "W" Flag (1938) — Starting in 1938, a white flag with a blue "W" is raised above the scoreboard after a Cubs win — or a blue flag with a white "L" after a loss — so the surrounding neighborhood could know the result.
    • Seventh Inning Stretch (1982) — The tradition of singing "Take Me Out to the Ball Game" at Cubs home games began when Hall of Fame announcer Harry Caray arrived in 1982, and after his death, the tradition of a celebrity guest conductor began.
    • "Go, Cubs, Go!" (1984) — Written by Chicago folk singer Steve Goodman in 1984, the song has become the Cubs' official victory song, played immediately after every home win while fans sing along in the stands.
    • Throwing Back Home Run Balls — If a fan catches an opposing team's home run ball at Wrigley Field, it is customary to throw it back onto the field — a beloved tradition unique to Cubs culture.
    • The Bleacher Bums — The bleacher sections at Wrigley Field are general admission and home to the Cubs' most boisterous fans, known as the Bleacher Bums, creating a rowdy and energetic party atmosphere.
    • Wrigleyville — The streets surrounding the ballpark, known as Wrigleyville, buzz with activity on game days as fans clad in Cubs gear congregate at local bars and restaurants, making the neighborhood itself a central part of the Cubs experience.
